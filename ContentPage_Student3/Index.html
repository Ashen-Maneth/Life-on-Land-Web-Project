All life on Earth depends on water. Drinking, cleaning, farming, transportation, generating energy, and recreation all require freshwater. Though they make up less than 1% of the earth's surface, freshwater ecosystems such as lakes, rivers, streams, and wetlands are home to about 10% of all known animal species and roughly 50% of all known fish species.


Wetlands, which are aquatic areas with a freshwater, saltwater, or mixed cover, are one of these freshwater habitats that are sometimes overlooked. They are the planet's natural carbon storage and waste-water treatment facilities. And they are essential to the security of food. There are between 300 million and 400 million people who depend on wetlands and live near them. They are in favor of rice farming, which is a staple food for half of the world's population. They also supply essential habitat, supplies, medications, clean water, protection from storms and shorelines, and flood management.


Despite the importance of wetlands to human populations, up to 87% of the world's wetlands have disappeared over the last 300 years, with the majority of this loss occurring around 1900. In order to preserve and safeguard these priceless habitats, WWF works.

1. What is a Wetland?


A wetland is an area of land that is continually or seasonally covered in water, whether it be fresh, salt, or somewhere in between. It operates as a separate ecology of its own. Wetland distinguishes itself from other land uses and water bodies mainly by its vegetation, which has adapted to the damp soil.




2. What kinds of species live in wetlands?


Wetlands are home to a diverse range of animals. During their lengthy migrations, wetlands serve as rest stops for a variety of birds, including sandpipers, kingfishers, ducks, and geese, giving them food and shelter. Wetlands provide food and refuge for a variety of mammals, including tigers, beavers, and otters. Naturally, marshes are home to a wide variety of fish.




3. What are some of the world's largest wetlands?


Among the world's largest wetlands are the Hudson Bay Lowland, the Amazon River Basin, and the West Siberian Lowland. Llanos de Moxos, in Bolivia, is the largest protected wetland in the world. It is more than 17 million acres, or almost the same as North Dakota.




4. How are wetlands natural water filters?


Wetlands alter nitrogen so that it is easier for plants to absorb, trap pollutants like phosphorus and heavy metals in their soils, and physically and chemically decompose bacteria. By acquiring and protecting $1.5 billion in land surrounding its upstate reservoirs, New York City discovered that it could avoid spending $3 billion to $8 billion on new wastewater treatment facilities.




5. What are the different types of wetlands??


Rivers, marshes, bogs, mangroves, mudflats, ponds, billabongs, lagoons, lakes, and floodplains are a few examples of wetlands. The majority of sizable wetland areas frequently have a variety of freshwater systems.




6. How do wetlands fight climate change??


Although they only make up a small percentage of the earth, wetlands are highly effective at absorbing carbon dioxide. They aid in preventing the release of the heat-trapping gas that fuels climate change from the atmosphere by storing 50 times more carbon than rain forests. Wetlands draw debris with a high carbon content, such as leaves and animal feces, down from the water's surface. The wetlands' natural debris is hidden by the sediment and water, keeping them hidden. Wetlands also have the advantage of growing swiftly and staying stable over long periods of time, which allows them to absorb carbon from the atmosphere.




7. What is the Freshwater Challenge?


A national program called the Freshwater Challenge seeks to mobilize the necessary resources to guarantee that 350 million hectares of degraded wetlands and 300,000 kilometers of degraded rivers would be restored by 2030. The United States accepted the Freshwater Challenge in December along with forty-three other nations!




8. What threats do wetlands face?


Development, agriculture, aquaculture, climate change, pollution, and dams are some of the issues facing wetlands. WWF promotes climate change adaptation and supports the Freshwater Challenge as part of its global efforts to protect wetlands.
